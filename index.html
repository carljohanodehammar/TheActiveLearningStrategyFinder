Field of study

This column shows the academic disciplines in which the strategy has been most commonly studied, tested, and reported in the research literature.

It does not mean the strategy only works in those fields. Most active learning strategies are transferable. A field label of Medicine or STEM reflects where the strongest published evidence comes from, not where the strategy is restricted to.

Strategies labelled Any have broad evidence across multiple disciplines or have been validated across diverse higher education contexts.

TPACK: Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge

TPACK is a framework developed by Mishra and Koehler (2006) describing the knowledge a teacher needs to teach effectively with technology. It identifies seven overlapping knowledge domains:

CK Content Knowledge: subject-matter expertise.
PK Pedagogical Knowledge: teaching and learning methods.
TK Technological Knowledge: using and adapting tools.
PCK Pedagogical Content Knowledge: teaching strategies for specific content.
TCK Technological Content Knowledge: how technology reshapes content.
TPK Technological Pedagogical Knowledge: technology enabling new approaches.
TPACK Full integration of all three.

The TPACK tags on each strategy show which knowledge domains a teacher primarily draws on when implementing it.

This column suggests how generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Copilot) could be integrated into the strategy, either by the teacher in preparation or by students as part of the activity itself.

These are illustrative possibilities, not requirements. AI integration should always be guided by your course's learning outcomes, your institution's policies, and transparent communication with students about when and how AI is being used.

SOLO taxonomy

The Structure of Observed Learning Outcomes (Biggs & Collis, 1982) describes five levels of increasing structural complexity in student understanding:

Prestructural: no relevant understanding yet.
Unistructural: one relevant element identified.
Multistructural: several elements, not yet connected.
Relational: elements connected into a coherent whole.
Extended abstract: understanding generalised beyond the given context.

Each strategy is tagged with the primary SOLO level it is designed to elicit. Simpler check tasks tend to operate at Unistructural or Multistructural level; richer activities that require connection-making and transfer tend to reach Relational or Extended abstract.

The level describes the intended cognitive demand of the strategy, not an absolute ceiling. Implementation and task design always matter.

Bloom's taxonomy

Bloom's revised taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) identifies six levels of cognitive processing, from lower-order to higher-order thinking:

Remember: recall facts and basic concepts.
Understand: explain ideas in your own words.
Apply: use information in a new situation.
Analyse: draw connections, break down components.
Evaluate: justify a decision or course of action.
Create: produce something new from parts.

Each strategy is tagged with the primary cognitive level it is designed to activate. This reflects the dominant demand of the strategy in a typical implementation. Many strategies touch multiple levels; the tag marks the primary target.
